Research

I am currently a graduate student in the DeMille group in the Department of Physics at the University of Chicago. We are a group of experimental atomic, molecular, and optical (AMO) physicists who are broadly interested in using cold and ultracold molecules for applications in quantum science and precision probes of physics beyond the Standard Model.

I currently work on the strontium monofluoride (SrF) direct laser cooling experiment. Here, we start with a cryogenic buffer gas beam source of SrF molecules at 4 K. Using laser slowing and cooling, we load roughly 104 molecules into a magneto-optical trap (MOT). We subsequently load roughly 100 molecules into an optical dipole trap (ODT) at roughly 10 μK. Pending further improvements to molecular laser cooling, slowing, and trapping, this serves as the starting point for further studies of the dynamics of an ultracold bulk gas of bosonic polar molecules.

Previously, during my undergraduate studies, I worked on short-term research projects in fluid dynamics (self-similarity in turbulent boundary layer flow), computational astrophysics (dark matter halo growth), computational soft matter physics (polymer crystallization), and experimental cosmology (Atacama Cosmology Telescope and Simons Observatory). I entered the field of experimental AMO physics research in Fall 2019, when I did my bachelor’s thesis with the Bakr group at Princeton University on assembly of ultracold bialkali sodium-rubidium (NaRb) molecules from laser-cooled Na and Rb atoms.

See Miscellaneous Writings for more details of my previous resesarch projects.
